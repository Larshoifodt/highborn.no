The Pentagon's fight with Anthropic was the first real test for how we will control powerful AI. The bad news: we all failed.

By Jeremy Kahn  ·  March 3, 2026  ·  8 min read

In February 2026, a simmering dispute between Anthropic and the U.S. Department of Defense suddenly boiled over into one of the most consequential confrontations between a tech company and the American government in recent memory.

It began when reports emerged that Claude, Anthropic's AI model, had been used through contractor Palantir to help locate and capture Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro — potentially without Anthropic's full knowledge or consent. The Pentagon, emboldened, demanded Anthropic remove all ethical guardrails from its military contracts.

Secretary of War Pete Hegseth labeled Anthropic a "supply chain risk to national security" — a designation historically reserved for foreign adversaries like China and Russia, never before applied to an American company.

The designation carried an immediate and devastating consequence: no contractor, supplier, or partner doing business with the U.S. military could conduct any commercial activity with Anthropic. The order took effect immediately, threatening the company's $200 million Pentagon contract and its relationships across the entire defense-industrial base.

Anthropic had held firm on two non-negotiable red lines throughout months of negotiations. CEO Dario Amodei called them "narrow exceptions" the company would never compromise on.

"We do not believe that today's frontier AI models are reliable enough to be used in fully autonomous weapons," Anthropic stated. The company also refused to allow Claude to be used for mass domestic surveillance of Americans.

The legal battle moved quickly. A federal judge initially blocked the designation as unconstitutional — a First Amendment victory for Anthropic. But an appeals court reversed course in April, upholding the Pentagon's authority and leaving Anthropic in legal limbo.

The geopolitical reverberations were swift. On the same evening Anthropic was blacklisted, a rival made its move.

Hours after Anthropic was declared a security risk, OpenAI announced its own deal with the Department of War — with far fewer guardrails. Pentagon then signed agreements with eight major tech companies, pointedly leaving Anthropic out.

The message to the AI industry could not have been clearer: ethical limits are a competitive liability. Sam Altman observed: "A really important point — we are not elected. I really don't want us to decide what to do if a nuke is coming towards the US."
